Green's Function and Scattering

Suppose we have a homogeneous linear differential equation: $$\mathcal{L}[y, \dot{y}, \dots] = 0$$ Its Green's function $G(x-x')$ is then the solution to the differential equation: $$\mathcal{L}[G(x, x')] = \delta(x-x')$$ Why is this useful? Suppose we want to solve the differential equation: $$\mathcal{L}[y, \dot{y}, \dots] = f(x)$$ We can take the definition of $G$ and drop integrals with respect to f(x') on both sides to get: $$\int f(x') \mathcal{L}[G(x, x')] \,\text{d}x' = \int f(x') \delta(x-x') \,\text{d}x'$$ However, since $\mathcal{L}$ is a linear operator that acts on $x$ and not $x'$, we can bring the integral on the right hand side into the operator $\mathcal{L}$. The right hand side is simply $f(x)$. This gives: $$\mathcal{L}[\int f(x') G(x, x') \,\text{d}x'] = f(x)$$ That tells us the solution to the above differential equation is: $$y(x) = \mathcal{L}[\int f(x') G(x, x') \,\text{d}x']$$ Therefore, the solution of an inhomogeneous linear differential equation is the convolution of the driving term $f(x)$ with the Green's function. In other words, the Green's function can be thought of as the response of our system due to a perturbation at $x'$.

How does it show up in the context of scattering? Consider the free space Schrodinger equation: $$(H_0 - E) \psi(\vec{\bf{r}}) = 0$$ If we add in a scattering potential, then the equation becomes: $$(H_0 - E) \psi(\vec{\bf{r}}) = V(\vec{\bf{r}})\psi(\vec{\bf{r}})$$ We can treat the right hand side of the equation as a "driving" term or a perturbation to our original free space equation. Note that in this case, the right-hand side perturbation depends on our solution $\psi(\vec{\bf{r}})$, so the situation is slightly different from above, and we will end up with an integral equation for $\psi(\vec{\bf{r}})$ instead of a direct solution.

Like before, we define the Green's function solution by: $$(H_0 - E) G(\vec{\bf{r}}-\vec{\bf{r}}') = \delta(\vec{\bf{r}}-\vec{\bf{r}}')$$ The solution is given by: $$\psi(\vec{\bf{r}}) = \psi_0(\vec{\bf{r}}) + \int G(\vec{\bf{r}}-\vec{\bf{r}}') V(\vec{\bf{r}}')\psi(\vec{\bf{r}'}) \,\text{d}^3\vec{\bf{r}}'$$ where $\psi_0(\vec{\bf{r}})$ is the free space solution. We can then solve for $\psi(\vec{\bf{r}})$ using an iterative approach.

The reason we want to do this instead of solving the Schrodinger equation directly is because this method produces physical solutions. The reason is that solutions to $(H_0 - E) G(\vec{\bf{r}}, \vec{\bf{r}}') = \delta(\vec{\bf{r}}-\vec{\bf{r}}')$ are not unique. There are two reasons. First, if we do not restrict $G$ to be a function of $\vec{\bf{r}}-\vec{\bf{r}}'$, then the general solution will be a combination of incoming/outgoing waves and plane waves corresponding to space solutions. Even if we enforce this restriction, $G(\vec{\bf{r}}-\vec{\bf{r}}')$ will still have degenerate solutions corresponding to incoming/outgoing waves. If we attempted to solve the Schrodinger equation directly, our solver may converge towards a solution that is a mixture of all of these waves, which is bad since we are dealing with a scattering problem a single well defined incoming state and a superposition of outgoing spherical waves. In addition, the presence of plane wave components in the final solution will mess up our boundary condition.

In our integral equation below this physical constraint is enforced because we only pick the $G(\vec{\bf{r}}-\vec{\bf{r}}')$ corresponding to outgoing waves, so our final solution will always be a single incoming wave plus a bunch of outgoing waves - therefore it remains physical. In addition, it has a nice analytical solution called the Born series.
